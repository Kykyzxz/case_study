THE STARRY NIGHT

Vincent van Gogh

1889 | Post-Impressionism

Oil on canvas, 73.7 cm × 92.1 cm

The Starry Night (1889) by Vincent van Gogh is widely considered the culmination of his artistic genius and emotional depth, representing one of the most iconic works in the history of Western art. Painted during his stay at the asylum of Saint-Rémy-de-Provence, the piece depicts a sweeping view of the night sky filled with swirling stars, a luminous crescent moon, and a tranquil village below, all rendered through the artist’s distinctive, impassioned brushstrokes and bold use of color. The dynamic sky, alive with motion and energy, mirrors the artist’s mental state, reflecting his struggle with isolation and his yearning for peace and spiritual understanding. The towering cypress tree in the foreground serves as a bridge between the earthly and the celestial, suggesting a connection between human existence and the infinite vastness of the universe. Through this fusion of realism and imagination, Van Gogh transforms a simple nocturnal scene into a profound emotional and philosophical vision, inviting viewers to feel the awe, mystery, and beauty of the cosmos as he experienced it.

CAFÉ TERRACE AT NIGHT

Vincent van Gogh

1888 | Post-Impressionism

Oil on canvas, 80.7 cm × 65.3 cm

CAFÉ TERRACE AT NIGHT (1888) by Vincent van Gogh is a radiant example of his Post-Impressionist style and his early fascination with the interplay of light and color. Painted in Arles, France, this work captures a charming outdoor café illuminated by the warm glow of lanterns under a deep, starlit sky. The golden yellows of the café contrast vividly with the cool blues and purples of the night, creating a scene that feels both lively and serene. The cobblestone street, dotted with figures enjoying the evening, leads the viewer’s eye into the depth of the composition, where the soft light of surrounding buildings and the subtle sparkle of constellations evoke a sense of warmth and wonder. Through expressive brushwork and vibrant color contrasts, Van Gogh transforms an ordinary street corner into a poetic vision of nighttime harmony, showcasing his ability to infuse emotion and atmosphere into everyday scenes. Café Terrace at Night not only reflects Van Gogh’s technical mastery but also reveals his deep appreciation for the beauty of nocturnal life, marking a pivotal moment in his artistic journey that would later culminate in the celestial brilliance of The Starry Night.

STARRY NIGHT OVER THE RHÔNE

Vincent van Gogh

1888 | Post-Impressionism

Oil on canvas, 72.5 cm × 92 cm

STARRY NIGHT OVER THE RHÔNE (1888) by Vincent van Gogh is a mesmerizing exploration of light, reflection, and human emotion, painted during his time in Arles, France. Created shortly before Café Terrace at Night, this masterpiece captures the tranquil beauty of the Rhône River illuminated by the soft radiance of starlight and the glimmering reflections of gas lamps along the distant shoreline. Van Gogh’s dynamic brushstrokes and rich palette of deep blues, golds, and greens create a sense of movement and depth, blending serenity with an underlying emotional intensity. The calm surface of the water mirrors the vast night sky, while the inclusion of a strolling couple in the foreground introduces a tender, human element that contrasts with the grandeur of the celestial expanse. Starry Night Over the Rhône stands as a testament to Van Gogh’s ability to merge technical mastery with profound emotion, forming a spiritual and visual bridge to the cosmic vision he would later realize in The Starry Night.

AT THE MOULIN ROUGE

Henri de Toulouse-Lautrec

1892–1895 | Post-Impressionism

Oil on canvas, 123 cm × 141 cm

At the Moulin Rouge (1892–1895) by Henri de Toulouse-Lautrec captures the vibrant yet haunting atmosphere of Parisian nightlife during the Belle Époque. Set inside the famed cabaret, the painting presents a striking composition filled with dancers, performers, and socialites illuminated by artificial light, reflecting the artist’s fascination with modern urban life. Through bold color contrasts, dynamic angles, and expressive brushwork, Toulouse-Lautrec conveys both the energy and melancholy that permeated the entertainment world of Montmartre. The greenish glow on the central female figure’s face creates a ghostly effect, symbolizing the tension between glamour and decadence, joy and isolation. As both participant and observer of the Parisian bohemian scene, Toulouse-Lautrec brought an unparalleled authenticity to his portrayal of the city’s nightlife, transforming fleeting social moments into lasting expressions of human emotion and modern experience. At the Moulin Rouge remains one of his most celebrated works, encapsulating the spirit of late 19th-century Paris with psychological depth and visual daring.

THE BLUE VASE

Paul Cézanne

1885–1887 | Post-Impressionism

Oil on canvas, 73 cm × 60 cm

The Blue Vase (1885–1887) by Paul Cézanne is a masterful still life that exemplifies the artist’s revolutionary approach to composition, color, and form. In this work, Cézanne transcends traditional realism by constructing objects through deliberate geometric shapes and carefully modulated tones, giving the scene both structure and harmony. The rich blues of the vase contrast elegantly with the warm hues of surrounding fruits and drapery, creating a balanced interplay of light and shadow that conveys depth without relying on conventional perspective. Every brushstroke is intentional, contributing to a rhythmic arrangement that reveals Cézanne’s analytical study of nature and his desire to capture its underlying permanence. Rather than merely representing objects, he sought to express their essence — their solidity and spatial relationships — laying the groundwork for the emergence of Cubism and modern art. The Blue Vase stands as a testament to Cézanne’s profound influence on the evolution of painting, uniting observation and abstraction in a composition of quiet yet powerful beauty.

THE SCREAM

Edvard Munch

1893 | Expressionism

Oil, tempera, and pastel on cardboard, 91 cm × 73.5 cm

The Scream (1893) by Edvard Munch is one of the most iconic and emotionally charged works in modern art, encapsulating the universal feeling of existential dread and psychological anguish. Painted during a period of profound personal and emotional unrest, Munch sought to convey not a literal scene but an inner vision — what he described as “the scream of nature.” The artwork portrays a lone, distorted figure standing on a bridge beneath a swirling, blood-red sky, its open mouth and hollow eyes expressing a primal cry of terror and despair. The undulating lines and vivid, unnatural colors amplify the sense of tension and instability, blurring the boundary between the figure and the turbulent landscape. Inspired by an episode in which Munch experienced an overwhelming wave of anxiety while walking at sunset near Oslofjord, the painting captures the intensity of human emotion as it merges with the forces of nature. Through this masterful fusion of psychological depth and expressive form, The Scream became a powerful symbol of modern alienation, profoundly influencing generations of artists and defining the visual language of existentialism.
